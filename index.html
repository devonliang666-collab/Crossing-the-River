The Project

Why We Built This

As two students from China, we grew up behind the Great Firewall, where information surrounding China's Cultural Revolution and Reform is tightly regulated. Western history classes gave us an intensely critical view — and as is the nature of institutionally told history, it is always self-serving. We took on this project to understand revolution and reform by building a version of the truth ourselves.

One of the most heartbreaking discoveries was the scale of suffering endured by common people. Under CCP rule, rural income was 134 yuan a year — roughly $80. Teenagers like us were denied college entrance exams and sent to farm communes under a program the government eventually abandoned. The Great Leap Forward and the Cultural Revolution played out the conflict between Marxist-Leninist ideology and pragmatic utilitarianism, producing suffering regardless of which side won. Despite all of it, some doctrinal policies worked. Such a complex and tragic story felt worth exploring.

"It has been almost thirty years since the founding of this country, and there are still people who want to eat. If we do not solve this problem, the peasants will revolt." — Xiao Donglian, quoted in Scene 1

The Performance

Ideology on Trial

We chose the performance format because the history spans three decades with the same two characters — a Reform Official and a Conservative Official — whose debate never fully resolves. We wanted to explore how there was no clear right or wrong: that policy was driven by ideological dogma or a desire to avoid past mistakes.

Both characters come from the historical record. Everything they say derives from actual speeches and debates. From our initial script, we learned to cut anachronisms: "GDP growth" became grain output and rural incomes; "meritocracy" was removed because the term wasn't coined until 1958.

Barry Naughton's research surprised us — we assumed Deng had a clear plan, but Naughton argues he was more of a politician than planner. That reframing changed Scene 2 from a confident reformist to one justifying policies by results. Shenzhen grew from 30,000 to a global city. The reforms succeeded — and yet: Wei Jingsheng served eighteen years in prison. Workers who built Shenzhen were denied income and permission to live there.

Reform without political freedom produces extraordinary prosperity and unresolved injustice simultaneously. All progress has a price, and who pays it is this performance's argument. — Process Paper

Research

Annotated Bibliography

Primary Sources
Chinese Grain Ration Coupons (Liangpiao), 1970s National Museum of American History, Smithsonian Institution. americanhistory.si.edu

Supports the Reform Official's poverty argument in Scene 1 — 134 yuan per year, rationed food, survival rather than prosperity.

People's Commune Organization, China, 1958 Asia for Educators, Columbia University. afe.easia.columbia.edu

Documents the commune system and supports the Conservative official's defense of collective equality in Scene 1.

1978 Yearly Calendar from Wuhan Tuberculosis Hospital National Library of Medicine, NIH. nlm.nih.gov

Visual context for the Conservative official's barefoot doctor argument in Scene 1.

Deng Xiaoping — "Emancipate the Mind, Seek Truth from Facts" Speech, Central Working Conference, December 13, 1978. marxists.org

Gave us the actual language Deng used to justify departing from Maoist orthodoxy. Supports Scene 2.

Hua Guofeng — Speech at the CCP Central Work Conference Wilson Center Digital Archive, translated by Caixia Lu. digitalarchive.wilsoncenter.org

Hua directly addresses the Two Whatevers policy and concedes its limitations — the Conservative's doctrine made concrete.

Mao Zedong — Quotations from Chairman Mao Tse-Tung Peking: Foreign Languages Press, 1966. marxists.org

The authoritative ideological source distributed to hundreds of millions of Chinese citizens.

Wei Jingsheng — "The Fifth Modernization: Democracy" Democracy Wall poster, December 5, 1978. afe.easia.columbia.edu

Posted the same week Deng's reforms were formalized. Deng had him arrested; Wei served eighteen years. Named directly in Scene 2.

Xiao Donglian — "On Reform and Opening Origins" robertsuettinger.com

Provided the direct quote used in Scene 1 about peasants and revolt, giving urgency to reform.

Secondary Sources
Andreas, Joel — "The Structure of Charismatic Mobilization" American Sociological Review, vol. 72, no. 3, 2007. doi.org

Documents the collapse of Chinese higher education during the Cultural Revolution. Supported the Conservative's gaokao defense.

Chow, Gregory C. — "China's Economic Transformation" China's 40 Years of Reform and Development, ANU Press, 2018. jstor.org

Argues reform was made inevitable by the Cultural Revolution's unpopularity; supports our portrayal of Deng as a cautious pragmatist.

Dikötter, Frank — Mao's Great Famine Walker & Company, 2010. (Winner, Samuel Johnson Prize)

Standard scholarly account estimating up to 45 million deaths. Directly supports the prologue's death toll.

Naughton, Barry — "Deng Xiaoping: The Economist" The China Quarterly, no. 135, 1993. jstor.org

Argues Deng was more politician than planner — changed how we wrote Scene 2 entirely.

Naughton, Barry — The Chinese Economy: Transitions and Growth MIT Press, 2007.

Standard Western academic text. Analysis of the dual-track system supports Scene 2's defense of gradual reform.

Vogel, Ezra F. — Deng Xiaoping and the Transformation of China Harvard University Press, 2011. (Winner, Lionel Gelber Prize)

The most comprehensive sympathetic scholarly portrait of Deng available in English. Directly supports the Reform Official's arguments.

Weber, Isabella M. — How China Escaped Shock Therapy Routledge, 2021.

Shows Chinese decision-makers consciously rejected shock therapy. The phrase "crossing the river by feeling the stones" examined here.

World Bank — "China Overview," 2023 worldbank.org

Confirms the 63% to 10% poverty reduction cited in Scene 3.

London School of Economics — Poverty and Inequality in China researchonline.lse.ac.uk

Provided the 134 yuan per year rural income figure that opens Scene 1.

Zhao, Dingxin — The Power of Tiananmen University of Chicago Press, 2001.

Standard analysis of the 1989 protests. Supported the Conservative's argument that opening the economy without political reform created the conditions for Tiananmen.
